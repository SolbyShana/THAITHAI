Start here. Numbers unlock markets, bills, songthaew prices — everything. Thai numbers follow a beautifully logical pattern: once you see it, you can build any price yourself.
🔊 How to read the pronunciation guides throughout this guide

Each phrase has a 🔊 strip written in plain English sounds — say it like you're just reading English. A few sounds to know:

ow = rhymes with "cow" (not "low")
ng at start = hum the "ng" of "sing"
bp / dt = soft sounds between b/p and d/t
aa = long "a" — hold it a beat longer
aw = as in "saw" or "law"
falling / rising = your voice drops or lifts at end

Money Phrases

Single most useful phrase
How much?
เท่าไหร่คะ
tâo rài khâ?
tow-rye kaa
▾

Point at what you want and say this. Tâo rài = how much. Khâ = polite ending for women (always add this). Locals use it constantly — it's direct and natural.

Chiang Mai note: You may also hear vendors say thao dai? — same meaning, slight Northern pronunciation. Both are perfect to use.
Buying
I'll take this one
เอาอันนี้ค่ะ
ao an níi khâ
ow-an-nee kaa
▾

Ao (ow, like "cow") = want / take. This is what Thais actually say — casual, natural, used everywhere. Point and say it.

That one (further away)
เอาอันนั้น
ao an nán
ow-an-nun
No thanks / I don't want it
ไม่เอา
mâi ao
my-ow

เอาสองอัน

Gentle negotiating
Can you come down a little?
ลดนิดนึงได้ไหมคะ
lót nít neung dâi mǎi khâ?
lote-nit-neung-dye-my kaa
▾

Lót = reduce. Nít neung = a tiny bit. This softer version is more natural than just lót dâi mǎi — the nít neung (just a tiny bit) shows you're being gentle, not aggressive. Night markets and Warorot — yes. Food stalls and convenience stores — no.

Always smile. The worst answer is mâi dâi (can't) — just smile and say mâi bpen rai (no problem) and move on.
Paying
Bill please
เก็บด้วยนะคะ
gèp dûay ná khâ
gep-doo-ay na kaa
▾

Literally "collect (the money) please." This is what locals actually say — more natural than chék bin (check bin). The ná makes it warm and friendly rather than abrupt.

Check bin (also used, more formal)
เช็คบิลด้วย
chék bin dûay
chek-bin-doo-ay
Keep the change
ไม่ต้องทอน
mâi dtông tɔɔn
my-dtong-tawn
Do you have change for 1000?
มีทอนพันไหมคะ
mii tɔɔn pan mǎi khâ?
mee-tawn-pun-my kaa
Warorot (Kad Luang), Sunday Walking Street, your local talaat — these phrases get you through any Chiang Mai market. You know ไก่ gài and หมู mǔu — let's build from there.
🌸 Chiang Mai market note: Northern vendors may have a slight Lanna accent — vowels can sound slightly rounder. Don't worry, they understand all Thai accents perfectly and will love that you're trying.

Food & Café Ordering

The Core Ordering Framework

Order anything
Can I get a... please
ขอ... หน่อยนะคะ
kɔ̌ɔ ... nòi ná khâ
kor ... noy-na kaa
▾

Kɔ̌ɔ (kor) = "may I have." Nòi softens it. Ná makes it warm rather than blunt. This is the real template — natural and used by Thais constantly.

Social & Everyday Life

The single most important thing to learn: ค่ะ

Add this to the end of almost everything you say as a woman. It signals respect and warmth — using it alone will open more doors than any other vocabulary. It also means "yes" on its own when someone asks you something.

ค่ะ
khâ
kaa (voice falls)
For statements and at end of sentences. Your voice drops slightly. "Yes" on its own.
คะ
khá
ka (voice rises)
For questions only. Voice lifts at end. "Really?" / "Oh yes?"
🌸 Chiang Mai local note: Locals here often use jâo (จ้าว) — a Northern Thai / Kham Mueang polite particle — instead of ค่ะ/ครับ. You'll hear it constantly. You don't need to use it yourself, but when you hear it, it's always friendly and warm.

Tones — The Quick Guide

Thai has 5 tones — don't panic. Focus on these patterns first.
➡️MidFlat — no movement at all. Many common words. Relax your voice.
📉LowSlightly lower than normal. Mâi (not) — say it a touch low and steady.
📈RisingStart low, go up — like asking a question. Mǎi (question word) goes up at the end.
🔻FallingStart higher, drop fast. Khâ (women's particle) — say it and let your voice fall.
🔼HighHigher pitch, hold it. Stretched and bright — like máa (but context will usually save you!).
Real talk: Tones matter, but context + smiling + trying = locals will understand you. You'll absorb tones naturally just from listening. Don't let tone anxiety stop you from speaking.

Resources — Curated for You

What You Have — How to Actually Use It

✅ App you have
Memrise — Use it right
Don't use the generic Thai beginner course — it teaches random vocabulary you won't use for months. Instead: search "Thai survival phrases" or "Thai food ordering" in Community Courses. Look for courses with audio recordings by native speakers — Memrise's real strength is audio + spaced repetition, which is exactly what you need for tones.
→ Best use: 5 minutes before bed. Let it quiz you on the week's phrases. Always turn audio on and say each word aloud — don't just tap through.
✅ App you have
Quizlet — Build your own sets
Quizlet is most powerful when you build your own sets from what you're actually learning. Each week, take phrases from this guide and create a set: English / Thai script / romanisation / pronunciation note. Use "Learn" mode, not just flashcards — it tests you multiple ways. You can also search for shared sets: "Thai market vocabulary" or "Thai café ordering."
→ Best use: Build one set per week (takes 10 min). Test yourself on it for 5 min daily until it's automatic.

Add These to Your Toolkit

🎧 Audio — Free
Pimsleur Thai — First 5 lessons free
Audio-only method where you listen and repeat — perfect for tones because you're training your ear and mouth together. The first 5 lessons cover greetings, basic questions, and numbers. Do a lesson while walking to a café or market. 30 minutes per lesson but it doesn't feel long.
→ Best for: your longer sessions (30+ min days). One lesson per week as a supplement to this guide.
📱 Tool — Free
Google Translate Camera Mode
Point your phone at Thai menus, market signs, price boards. Not perfect but genuinely useful — and over time it helps your brain start recognising Thai script shapes. Save interesting words to your Quizlet set as you go. A habit worth building at every new restaurant or stall.
→ Use every time you're somewhere new. It makes the language feel less foreign and more readable.
📺 YouTube — Free
"Learn Thai with Mod"
Short 5–10 minute videos focused on specific situations — market shopping, ordering food, café visits. Native speaker who explains cultural context as well as the words. Far better than long generic courses. ThaiPod101 also has useful situational content — skip their long intros.
→ Watch one video matching your current week's focus. Pause and repeat phrases aloud. Passive watching doesn't stick — active repetition does.
🗣️ In Chiang Mai — Your fastest route
Language Exchange
One hour per week of real conversation with a Thai person will accelerate you faster than any app. Look in Facebook groups: "Chiang Mai Language Exchange" and "Chiang Mai Expats & Locals." You help them with English; they help you with Thai. Your yoga studio, regular café, and market vendors are also natural practice partners — they love the effort and will remember you for it.
→ Aim for one session per week from month 2 onwards. Even 30 minutes is transformative.
📚 When you're ready to go further
AUA Language Centre, Chiang Mai
One of Thailand's most respected language schools — right here in the city. They use an immersive method (ALG — Automatic Language Growth) where you absorb Thai naturally without direct translation. Excellent for auditory learners. Flexible short courses and drop-ins available.
→ You don't need this yet. Bookmark it for month 2–3 when you want to go beyond survival phrases.

Your Daily 15-Minute Structure

0–5 min · Review
Say yesterday's phrases aloud — not silently. Your mouth needs the practice as much as your brain.
5–12 min · New content
Learn 3–5 phrases from this guide for the week. Read the pronunciation guide and say each one 3x out loud.
12–15 min · App quiz
Memrise or Quizlet — let it test you. Don't skip. This is what moves words from short-term to long-term memory.
The real secret: Use one new phrase in the real world every single day. Even just kawp-khoon-maak na khâ to someone who helped you. Real use beats 10x more app time — every time.
